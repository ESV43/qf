Part 1: Ontological Models & Generalized Contextuality

Ontological Models and Realist Theories

For any realist theory, the probability of an outcome $z$ given a preparation $x$ and measurement $y$ is given by an integral over hidden variables (ontic states) $\lambda$:

$$ p(z|x,y) = \int \mu(\lambda|P_x) \xi(z|M_y, \lambda) d\lambda $$

where:

  • $P_x$ is the preparation procedure.
  • $\mu(\lambda|P_x)$ is the probability distribution over $\lambda$ for preparation $P_x$.
  • $M_y$ is the measurement procedure.
  • $\xi(z|M_y, \lambda)$ is the probability of outcome $z$ for measurement $M_y$ given ontic state $\lambda$. This is often called the response function.

If $\xi(z|M_y, \lambda) \in \{0,1\}$, the model is said to be deterministic.

Generalized Notion of Contextuality (Spekkens 2005)

Preparation Equivalence and Non-Contextuality

Two preparations $P_x$ and $P_{x'}$ are equivalent (denoted $P_x \sim P_{x'}$) if for all outcomes $z$ and all measurements $M_y$, the probabilities are the same:

$$ p(z|P_x, M_y) = p(z|P_{x'}, M_y) $$

This means they produce the same observable statistics for all possible measurements.

Preparation Non-Contextuality (PNC): If $P_x \sim P_{x'}$, then their corresponding distributions over ontic states must be identical: $$ \mu(\lambda|P_x) = \mu(\lambda|P_{x'}) \quad \forall \lambda $$

Furthermore, if a preparation $P_x$ is equivalent to a convex mixture of other preparations, e.g., $P_x \sim q P_1 + (1-q) P_2$, then under PNC, its ontic state distribution is the corresponding convex mixture:

$$ \mu(\lambda|P_x) = q \mu(\lambda|P_1) + (1-q) \mu(\lambda|P_2) $$

Measurement Equivalence and Non-Contextuality

Two measurements $M_y$ and $M_{y'}$ are equivalent (denoted $M_y \sim M_{y'}$) if for all outcomes $z$ and all preparations $P_x$, the probabilities are the same:

$$ p(z|P_x, M_y) = p(z|P_x, M_{y'}) $$

This means they yield the same statistics when applied to any possible preparation.

Measurement Non-Contextuality (MNC): If $M_y \sim M_{y'}$, then their response functions must be identical for any given ontic state:

$$ \xi(z|\lambda, M_y) = \xi(z|\lambda, M_{y'}) \quad \forall z, \lambda $$

The response function for an outcome is independent of the "context" of other co-measurable observables, as long as the overall measurements are statistically equivalent.

Relationship to Kochen-Specker (KS) Non-Contextuality

Kochen-Specker (KS) non-contextuality is a special case of measurement non-contextuality. KS typically applies to scenarios where:

  • Measurements are projective (describe by sets of orthogonal projectors).
  • The underlying ontological model is deterministic, i.e., $\xi(z|M,\lambda) \in \{0,1\}$.

In the KS sense, if an observable (represented by a projector $\Pi_k = \ket{\psi_k}\bra{\psi_k}$) can be part of two different complete sets of compatible observables (contexts) $M$ and $\tilde{M}$, then the value assigned to it by the hidden variable $\lambda$ must be the same regardless of the context: $$ \xi(\text{outcome for }\Pi_k | M, \lambda) = \xi(\text{outcome for }\Pi_k | \tilde{M}, \lambda) $$

Contextuality Inequality Example (Spekkens)

Quantum theory can be shown to be preparation-contextual by violating inequalities derived from the assumption of PNC. Consider preparations $P_1, P_2, P_3, P_4$ and measurements $M_0, M_1$. Assume the statistical equivalence:

$$ \frac{1}{2}P_1 + \frac{1}{2}P_2 \sim \frac{1}{2}P_3 + \frac{1}{2}P_4 $$

If PNC holds, then $\frac{1}{2}\mu(\lambda|P_1) + \frac{1}{2}\mu(\lambda|P_2) = \frac{1}{2}\mu(\lambda|P_3) + \frac{1}{2}\mu(\lambda|P_4)$.

Define an expression $I$ based on various outcome probabilities:

\begin{align*} I = & p(+1|P_1, M_0) + p(+1|P_2, M_1) + p(-1|P_3, M_0) + p(+1|P_3, M_1) \\ & + p(+1|P_4, M_0) + p(-1|P_4, M_1) \\ & \quad (\text{Note: This specific I form may vary, adapted from general structure in notes}) \end{align*}

In any preparation non-contextual theory satisfying the equivalence, it can be shown that $I \le 6$. However, quantum mechanics predicts a value that can exceed this, for example, $I = 4+2\sqrt{2} \approx 6.828$, thus demonstrating that quantum theory is preparation-contextual.

For instance, using $P_1 = \ket{0}\bra{0}$, $P_2 = \ket{1}\bra{1}$, $P_3 = \ket{+}\bra{+}$, $P_4 = \ket{-}\bra{-}$, and $M_0 = \sigma_z$, $M_1 = \sigma_x$. The quantum calculation involves $p(\text{outcome}|P_i, M_j) = \text{Tr}(\Pi_{\text{outcome}}^{M_j} P_i)$.

Questions & Answers

Realist theories postulate the existence of "ontic states" (often called hidden variables, denoted by $\lambda$) that provide a complete description of a physical system. The probabilities of measurement outcomes are then derived by averaging over the possible ontic states, weighted by a distribution function specific to the system's preparation.

It's given by the integral $p(z|x,y) = \int \mu(\lambda|P_x) \xi(z|M_y, \lambda) d\lambda$, where $\mu(\lambda|P_x)$ is the distribution of ontic states for preparation $P_x$, and $\xi(z|M_y, \lambda)$ is the response function giving the probability of outcome $z$ for measurement $M_y$ given ontic state $\lambda$.

PNC states that if two preparation procedures $P_x$ and $P_{x'}$ are operationally indistinguishable (i.e., they yield the same statistics for all possible measurements), then they must correspond to the exact same probability distribution over the underlying ontic states $\lambda$. That is, if $P_x \sim P_{x'}$, then $\mu(\lambda|P_x) = \mu(\lambda|P_{x'})$.

KS contextuality is a specific type of measurement non-contextuality. It applies to deterministic ontological models where an observable is assigned a definite value by the ontic state, independent of which other compatible observables are measured alongside it. Generalized contextuality broadens this concept to non-deterministic models and also introduces the notion of preparation contextuality.
